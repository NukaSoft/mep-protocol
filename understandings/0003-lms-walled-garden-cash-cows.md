# 0003: LMS Platforms Are Walled Garden Cash Cows

## The Understanding
> The entire LMS industry sells convenience at 100x markup — actual COGS is ~$50-100/month on open source, but platforms charge millions in aggregate because there's no API layer, no migration path, and no automation. AI + open source kills the switchboard.

## Raw Thoughts
> So after a period of time, trying to figure out how to monetize my brain in using
> podcasting and ultimately ending up on the content curation school bandwagon with Kajabi
> and LMS systems. I learned quickly that programs like Microsoft — we use at Microsoft,
> Tick was one of our CMS/LMS systems — how LMS systems are so disjointed. There's no
> migration path from one to the other. Once you commit to one platform, you are screwed.
> None of your content is available later on. It is effectively an entirely manual process.
> The onboarding of courseware and building a curated course — because there's not an API
> layer in any of this. It is all done through the user interface, which makes the curation
> process incredibly bespoke and crafted for every class. There literally is no automation
> in any of the tools that are available right now.
>
> And so because that complexity is so opaque — of what actually has to happen underneath
> the screen — it seems like magic to somebody like a teacher or individual person that's
> trying to set up a side hustle. So the active commitment on the sales side is really
> high. You have to create an account. You have to put money down. And once you go down
> the road, you are seductively brought into a system that the only way that you can
> maintain any continued revenue is by being at the highest level of like $200+ a month.
> Once you get any traction.
>
> Those systems also reward — and they're all built for viral. They're not built for
> individual side hustlers or a church or somebody like that trying to facilitate something,
> or a civil group trying to do onboarding and training with somebody. No. If you're in
> corporate America, you have to go sign your soul up to the devil to one of these
> platforms. And then at scale, enterprises pay millions for this stuff. And somebody like
> School or Kajabi at the low end, those systems — they scale really quickly. The
> onboarding to production part is quick, however, and really meant for nontechnical

>
> And so that's why somebody like Alex Hormozi sells success. But when you look in his

> the creative content that he has, but it's by inspiring people to be on his platform.

> And after running open source and looking through all the technical detail, effectively,
> an entire LMS system on open source on a well-supported platform — cost of goods is

> is crazy inexpensive. So for — he has literally a million people on that platform paying,

>
> And that's the moment — this is totally a scam. Not a scam, but a locked-in ecosystem
> walled garden. That once you get in, you cannot get out. That you're trading convenience
> for cost — actually, not just privacy, but cost. Even a closed system like School is an
> insignificantly inexpensive thing to host. So it's really the software — which Claude or
> any other element, or the other AI given Chrome control, with or even without an API,

>
> The impact is that once realized, the cost of training somebody goes way down. The
> friction between a content creator and the actual delivery of the content is close to
> zero as possible. And will continue to go down. And so all of the stuff in the middle
> that can't be controlled through an API is gonna get tossed. People are so pissed
> already at these. And as they've grown, post-COVID especially, to do everything online,
> this has now become an overwhelming cost and poorly attended and a really bad engagement
> platform. Keep enterprise customers off different things. You don't have the ability to
> do a lot of branding or even curating or inserting any of your own custom logic into
> that whole system because it is closed source. So the business value and time saved is
> once automated, not only will I eliminate hundreds, thousands of dollars a year in
> hosting fees. I will also eliminate the time that it takes to manage two different
> platforms to try to find output channels where the content is created in some other —
> this assumes I'm gonna create the content and log in there. But in fact, I don't wanna
> log in there as a content creator. I just want my customers to log in there, and I just
> want my AI on the back end just to go and manage the forum posts automatically. So this
> system will be and is going to be completely automated at some point in time.

## The Context
Pierre was reflecting on his experience with Kajabi, School (Alex Hormozi's platform), and enterprise LMS tools like Tick (used at Microsoft). He'd gone through the full cycle — podcasting, content curation, trying to monetize expertise — and hit the same wall every time: locked-in platforms with no API, no migration path, no automation, and costs that scale with success rather than infrastructure. The realization crystallized when he compared the actual hosting COGS (~$50-100/month on open source) against what platforms charge in aggregate (millions/month at ~1% COGS).

## The Implication
This is a Do Nothing Company product opportunity. An AI-managed open-source LMS — where content creators never log into the platform, only their customers do — eliminates the entire walled garden business model. Claude (or any AI with browser control) can manage courseware deployment, forum moderation, and student onboarding automatically. The consulting flywheel around LMS implementation and migration is dead. Pierre's classroom repo fork is the seed of this product. Combined with Understanding #0002 (Claude Code is an app builder), the path from "I have course content" to "students are learning" becomes a single repo with an AI backend.

## The Thread
Connects to #0002 (Claude Code is an app builder). This is the first concrete product that proves the thesis: the classroom repo, managed by AI, hosted for pennies, delivered as a React frontend. Opens a new sub-thread: "walled garden disruption" — where else does the same pattern apply? (CRMs, project management tools, email marketing platforms — anywhere convenience is sold at 100x infrastructure cost with no migration path.)

## Phoenix Impact
Kills two things:
1. **LMS hosting fees** — $200+/month for Kajabi/School → $50-100/month on open source (70-95% cost reduction)
2. **Manual content management** — no more logging into two platforms, no more bespoke courseware curation through UIs. AI manages the entire pipeline from markdown content → deployed course → student engagement → forum moderation.

The consulting business around LMS implementation, migration, and management is also dead — AI handles what used to require a team.
